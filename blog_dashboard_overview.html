Building the SHSM Fraud Analytics Dashboard Suite

Bridging the Gap Between Public Data and Actionable Intelligence. How we transformed raw federal enforcement records into interactive insights—and what the numbers reveal about the evolving landscape of health care and securities fraud.

Introduction

Whistleblower and fraud enforcement data is technically public, but “public” does not mean “accessible.” The Department of Justice (DOJ) publishes annual False Claims Act statistics dating back to 1987. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) posts every Notice of Covered Action (NOCA) and final whistleblower order on its website.

However, the raw data is scattered across static spreadsheets, PDFs, and hundreds of individual press releases. It does not tell a story on its own. The goal of the SHSM Fraud Analytics project was to bridge this gap: to pull the data together, give it structure, and make it explorable.

The result is a suite of three interactive dashboards. This post explores how each was built, our key findings, and where we plan to take the analysis next.

Dashboard 1

DOJ False Claims Act Recovery

The Macro View: 38 Years of Federal Enforcement

The DOJ FCA Federal Fraud Recovery Dashboard tracks settlements and judgments from FY1987 through FY2025. This dashboard synthesizes the modern history of the False Claims Act—the federal government’s primary tool for recovering funds lost to fraud.

Key Insights

A New Record in 2025 FY2025 set an all-time record with $6.89 billion recovered, largely driven by a historic $5.7 billion from health care matters.
Health Care Dominance Since FY1997, health care fraud has accounted for the majority of total annual recoveries in every year except two. Conversely, defense procurement fraud—which once dominated the early stages of the program—has declined significantly as a percentage of the total.
The Qui Tam Engine Whistleblower-filed (qui tam) lawsuits remain the primary driver of enforcement. In FY2025, relators filed a record 1,297 new suits and relator-initiated suits accounted for nearly 90% of the DOJ’s total fraud recoveries.
Defense Fraud Pivot After near-zero DOD recoveries between 2019 and 2022, FY2025 saw a sudden surge to approximately $634 million. This suggests a meaningful pivot in DOJ enforcement priorities or the resolution of a significant multi-year investigative pipeline.

DOJ Health Care Fraud Recoveries

The Granular View: CY2024 Case-Level Analysis

We catalogued 110 individual FCA cases identified through a comprehensive review of DOJ’s national and regional press feeds. While this dataset provides a robust representative sample of CY2024 enforcement activity, it is limited to cases DOJ chose to publicize through formal press releases and may not capture every settlement reached at the local district level or those resolved without a public announcement.

Key Insights

Opioid & Drug Dispensing Recoveries CY2024 was a pivotal year for recovering funds related to the opioid epidemic, with settlements exceeding $1.1 billion. While significant, this represents a major outlier for the year and may not be indicative of recovery trends in subsequent cycles.
The “Bread and Butter” of Enforcement Overbilling and anti-kickback schemes remain the primary drivers of the FCA, accounting for over 60% of all cases. These categories represent the core of federal health care fraud enforcement actions.
Physicians vs. Systems While physicians and private practices are the most frequent defendants by count (29 cases), the largest dollar recoveries are consistently drawn from hospitals, health systems, laboratories, and pharmaceutical manufacturers—a reflection of the massive scale of institutional billing.
The “Whale” Effect The data highlights how a few massive resolutions can skew aggregate annual statistics. The $650 million McKinsey & Company settlement alone represented roughly 25% of all dollar recoveries tracked in our 2024 dataset.
Dashboard 3

SEC Whistleblower Program

Tracking the Tips-to-Orders Pipeline (2011–2026)

The SEC Whistleblower Program Dashboard tracks the full arc of the program from its 2011 inception through April 2026. It maps the relationship between tip volume and enforcement outputs (NOCAs and Final Orders).

Key Insights

The One-in-Three Award Rate While the SEC program maintains a cumulative 32% award rate, this average masks a sharp decline in recent success. The dashboard reveals that under the Atkins era, the award rate has plummeted to just over 10%—an indication of more frivolous award applications or changing priorities for the administration.
The Lagging Indicator SEC tips typically follow a multi-year trajectory from intake and investigation to final enforcement. Consequently, the record-breaking tip volumes of 2022–2024 will hopefully drive enforcement output through 2026 and beyond.
The 2021–2025 Productivity Peak Under the leadership of Acting Chair Lee and Chair Gensler, the SEC whistleblower program reached new heights in output. The agency averaged over four award orders per month, alongside record-breaking payouts and a consistently high volume of Notices of Covered Actions.

Future Exploration: From Descriptive to Predictive

The dashboards in their current form are descriptive. Our next priority is to add analytical layers that provide deeper value for understanding enforcement priorities and action.

01
FCA Case-Level Datasets. The CY2024 health care fraud dashboard covers one calendar year for one category of fraud. Creating a similar analysis for different fraud types—such as DOD procurement fraud or customs fraud—will provide a clearer picture of DOJ’s enforcement priorities.
02
Geographic Heat Mapping. Both DOJ and SEC press releases contain valuable location data, from specific U.S. Districts to states. Mapping this activity geographically will reveal which jurisdictions are the primary hubs for fraud enforcement and/or where the highest volumes of whistleblower tips are originating.
03
Mining SEC Final Orders. While the current dashboard tracks the volume of awards and denials, the next phase will go deeper into the text of the orders themselves. We plan to catalogue the specific grounds for SEC denials—such as “untimely filing” or the information did not “substantially contribute” to the covered action—to identify trends that can help whistleblowers avoid common pitfalls.
04
SEC Award Amount Analysis. While the current dashboard tracks the count of awards and denials, we are moving toward scraping and aggregating the specific dollar amounts published in final order notices. This will allow for an analysis of award size distribution and average award percentages relative to sanctions collected.

Conclusion & Action

Fraud enforcement data often suffers from a transparency problem—not because the data is hidden, but because it is too fragmented to be useful. These dashboards make the patterns of federal enforcement concrete, comparable, and actionable.

The findings reinforce several things that practitioners in this space understand intuitively: health care fraud dominates FCA enforcement; qui tam relators are the engine of recovery; the SEC’s award rate is well under 50%; and enforcement output in any given year reflects investigative decisions made years earlier. What the dashboards add is a visual and interactive layer that makes these patterns concrete, comparable, and—most importantly—arguable.
